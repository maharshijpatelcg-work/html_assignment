Mutual fund statistics

The first mutual fund in India was introduced in 1963 when the Government of India launched the Unit Trust of India (UTI). Mutual funds in India are broadly categorised into equity funds, hybrid funds, and debt funds.

As of 31 December 2023, total Assets Under Management (AUM) of the Indian mutual fund industry stood at ₹50.78 trillion (approximately US$600 billion).[2]

According to regulators and official reports, a majority of mutual fund units are redeemed within two years of investment, with a small percentage of units held beyond five years.[3]

Controversies

The mutual fund industry in India has experienced several high-profile crises and controversies, mainly related to liquidity stress, credit defaults, and governance issues. These events have often led to regulatory intervention and changes in industry practice.

List of Mutual fund companies/schemes bankrupted, defaulted or closed

2020 Franklin Templeton Mutual Fund fiasco

In April 2020, Franklin Templeton India wound up six credit funds with combined assets close to US$4 billion, citing a lack of liquidity amid the COVID-19 pandemic. SEBI later conducted a probe and imposed penalties and restrictions on the fund house.[15]

Reliance Mutual Fund

In 2019, certain debt schemes managed by Reliance Mutual Fund experienced a liquidity crisis driven by exposure to troubled companies such as DHFL, which led to heavy redemptions.

IL&FS crisis and impact

The 2018 IL&FS defaults triggered significant markdowns in NAVs of affected mutual fund schemes and highlighted the credit risk inherent in some debt funds. Regulators responded with stricter rules on issuer exposure and liquidity management.

Amtek Auto impact

Defaults by Amtek Auto in 2015 forced some fund managers to suspend redemptions and manage liquidity under stress, demonstrating the contagion risk of corporate defaults.

Birla Sun Life Mutual Fund

Aditya Birla Sun Life Mutual Fund faced redemption pressures in some debt schemes due to exposure to the Essel Group and related entities.

DHFL crisis and impact

Defaults at Dewan Housing Finance Corporation (DHFL) caused heavy losses for funds exposed to its debt, prompting regulatory scrutiny and changes in portfolio strategies across the industry.

2001 UTI Mutual Fund fiasco

The Unit Trust of India suffered a major crisis in 2001 due to redemption pressures and market manipulation, which led to a government-led restructuring of UTI.

DHFL Pramerica Mutual Fund

Joint ventures and schemes with exposure to DHFL were affected when the company defaulted, resulting in marked down NAVs and investor losses.

Yes Mutual Fund

Exposure to Yes Bank's troubled debt papers impacted several schemes and required write-downs when the bank faced severe financial distress.

Kotak Mutual Fund

Various legacy issues and scheme-specific stresses have at times affected Kotak-managed funds, leading to investor concern and regulatory engagement.

HDFC Mutual Fund

HDFC Mutual Fund has faced episodic pressures related to exposure in particular sectors or issuers, resulting in tighter risk controls.

Sahara Mutual Fund

Sahara and related entities have historically attracted regulatory attention for governance and investor protection reasons.

Market segment

The Indian mutual fund industry serves retail, HNI (high-net-worth individuals) and institutional investors through a range of equity, debt and hybrid products. Product design and distribution have evolved with increased participation by individual investors.

Average assets under management

Measures of AUM and the composition of assets give a snapshot of the size and structure of the industry. The share of household savings going into mutual funds has been growing, but bank deposits remain a dominant household savings vehicle.
